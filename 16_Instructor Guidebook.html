Instructor Guidebook

Medi-Cal Peer Support Specialist Training Program
80-Hour Core Training Curriculum

This guidebook provides instructional direction for faculty delivering the Rhombus University Peer Support Specialist Training Program. It outlines instructional expectations, facilitation standards, module-level teaching guidance, activity structure, learner support practices, and assessment administration procedures.

Purpose of the Guidebook

This guidebook is intended for lead instructors, peer co-instructors, adjunct faculty, and instructional support personnel delivering the Rhombus University Peer Support Specialist Training Program.

Its purpose is to preserve curriculum consistency while allowing instructors to teach with authenticity, clarity, and appropriate facilitation style.

Instructor Role

Instructors are responsible for:
  • delivering approved curriculum content
  • maintaining instructional quality and pacing
  • supporting learner engagement and safety
  • reinforcing peer role scope and professional standards
  • evaluating learner participation and competency development
  • protecting confidentiality and dignity within classroom dialogue

Instructional Philosophy

Instruction should be practical, respectful, structured, and recovery-oriented. Faculty should teach in a way that supports learner growth while maintaining professional expectations.

Instructors should consistently reinforce:
  • hope-centered language
  • person-driven support principles
  • clear role boundaries
  • non-coercive engagement
  • cultural humility
  • trauma-informed practice

Core Facilitation Standards

Standard Instructional Expectation
Respect Maintain a learning environment where every learner is treated with dignity
Structure Follow module sequence, time allocations, and core instructional objectives
Clarity Explain expectations, key concepts, and role distinctions plainly
Safety Support a trauma-informed environment that avoids unnecessary emotional exposure
Consistency Use approved curriculum materials and evaluation methods
Professionalism Model workforce-ready conduct and communication

General Instructional Sequence

Each instructional block should generally follow this sequence:

  1. Opening and framing
  2. Review of learning objectives
  3. Concept teaching
  4. Applied examples
  5. Interactive learner activity
  6. Reflection or discussion
  7. Knowledge check or summary

Opening Script Template

Suggested Opening Language:

“Welcome back. Today we are focusing on a core part of peer support practice. Our goal is to understand the concept clearly, apply it appropriately, and connect it to real service settings. Please stay grounded, respectful, and engaged as we work through the material together.”

Learner Discussion Guidance

Instructors should:
  • encourage participation without forcing disclosure
  • redirect when discussion becomes clinically inappropriate or unsafe
  • keep learner stories connected to training objectives
  • avoid allowing class time to become unstructured processing
  • reinforce that lived experience should serve learning and role development

Confidentiality Reminder Script

Suggested Language:

“As a reminder, personal experiences shared here stay here. We protect each other’s dignity and privacy. Please avoid sharing identifying information about clients, staff, agencies, or classmates.”

Module-by-Module Instructor Guidance

Module 1 – Foundations of Peer Support and Recovery

Instructor Focus

Establish the identity, purpose, and boundaries of peer support work. This is the foundation for the rest of the course.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • differentiate peer support from clinical care
  • define recovery, wellness, and resilience clearly
  • normalize learner uncertainty about the role
  • introduce purposeful self-disclosure as a professional tool

Recommended Activities

  • scope-of-practice sorting exercise
  • recovery language rewrite practice
  • group dialogue on hope and lived experience

Instructor Caution

Do not allow this module to become a storytelling session without structure. Keep stories brief, relevant, and connected to role development.

Module 2 – Advocacy, Rights, Consumers, and Families

Instructor Focus

Teach learners that peer advocacy supports self-determination, not control or rescuing behavior.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • differentiate speaking for someone from helping them speak for themselves
  • address stigma clearly and practically
  • help learners understand family voice without undermining client autonomy

Recommended Activities

  • rights and barriers mapping
  • stigma identification worksheet
  • self-advocacy role-play

Module 3 – Behavioral Health Foundations and Co-Occurring Conditions

Instructor Focus

Provide enough behavioral health literacy for learners to function responsibly without drifting into clinical interpretation.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • keep content broad and practical
  • reinforce role boundaries often
  • teach recovery pathways without creating diagnostic authority

Recommended Activities

  • case comparison exercises
  • co-occurring condition discussion
  • appropriate referral identification

Module 4 – Cultural Humility, Structural Competence, and Trauma-Informed Practice

Instructor Focus

Promote responsive, respectful peer service without moralizing or shaming learners for imperfect language or prior understanding.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • frame humility as an ongoing practice
  • teach trauma-informed principles concretely
  • connect structural barriers to navigation realities

Recommended Activities

  • bias reflection prompts
  • communication repair role-play
  • trauma-informed response examples

Instructor Caution

Avoid confrontational facilitation. Correct respectfully, clearly, and without humiliating learners.

Module 5 – Communication, Groups, Conflict Resolution, and Boundaries

Instructor Focus

This module is highly skill-based and should include live modeling, repeated practice, and immediate feedback.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • demonstrate active listening before asking learners to practice it
  • teach boundaries as trust-protection, not coldness
  • reinforce respectful de-escalation strategies

Recommended Activities

  • listening triads
  • group facilitation fishbowl
  • boundary red-flag analysis
  • conflict response role-play

Module 6 – Recovery Planning, Self-Awareness, and Self-Care

Instructor Focus

Help learners understand that peer support strengthens another person’s goals, not the helper’s agenda.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • teach strengths-based support
  • connect self-care to workforce sustainability
  • normalize self-awareness as professional discipline

Recommended Activities

  • strengths interview exercise
  • goal mapping worksheet
  • self-care planning template

Module 7 – Community Inclusion, Navigation, and Employment Readiness

Instructor Focus

Emphasize practical service navigation and workforce readiness. This module should feel concrete and action-oriented.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • teach warm handoffs, not passive referrals
  • connect inclusion to recovery support
  • reinforce professionalism and employability

Recommended Activities

  • resource mapping
  • warm handoff practice
  • mock interview questions
  • résumé discussion

Module 8 – Documentation, Confidentiality, Digital Literacy, and Team Practice

Instructor Focus

This content must remain clear, structured, and grounded in real workplace expectations.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • teach objective language
  • clarify what belongs in a peer support note
  • address privacy risks in digital environments
  • reinforce team communication boundaries

Recommended Activities

  • sample note correction
  • documentation lab
  • confidentiality scenario review
  • professional email or digital communication exercise

Module 9 – Safety, Crisis Planning, and Mandated Responsibilities

Instructor Focus

Help learners remain calm, aware, and appropriately role-bound in safety-related situations.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • teach recognition of warning signs
  • make escalation pathways concrete
  • emphasize that peers do not work alone in crisis situations
  • clarify mandated awareness and consultation duties

Recommended Activities

  • crisis scenario practice
  • safety planning exercise
  • escalation decision tree review

Module 10 – Integration, Skills Demonstration, Exam Preparation, and Transition to Work

Instructor Focus

This final module should consolidate learning, strengthen confidence, and confirm readiness.

Key Teaching Priorities

  • review core peer competencies
  • administer final applied assessments consistently
  • frame completion as professional transition, not just course closure

Recommended Activities

  • mock final review
  • skills demonstration stations
  • workforce transition discussion
  • professional development planning

Managing Difficult Classroom Moments

Situation Recommended Instructor Response
Oversharing or emotionally unsafe disclosure Thank the learner, contain the discussion, redirect to the topic, and offer private follow-up if needed
Conflict between learners Pause escalation, restate expectations, de-personalize the issue, and redirect to respectful engagement
Confidentiality concern Interrupt respectfully, stop identifying detail from continuing, and restate confidentiality standards
Scope confusion Clarify peer role boundaries and provide concrete examples of appropriate versus inappropriate actions
Learner disengagement Use structured participation prompts, pair work, or direct but respectful check-in

Assessment Administration Guidance

Instructors should ensure that assessments are:

  • administered consistently across cohorts
  • explained clearly before completion
  • scored using approved criteria
  • documented accurately
  • used to support growth as well as completion decisions

Feedback Guidance

Effective learner feedback should be:

  • specific
  • clear
  • behavior-based
  • respectful
  • connected to role expectations

Suggested Feedback Formula:

“What you did well was ____. One area to strengthen is ____. In peer support work, that matters because ____.”

End-of-Day Closing Script Template

Suggested Language:

“Today we covered material that connects directly to how peer support is practiced professionally. Please review your notes, reflect on what stood out, and come prepared to continue building skills in the next session.”

Instructor Documentation Checklist

  • attendance recorded
  • lesson delivered according to schedule
  • activities completed or documented if modified
  • assessment records updated
  • learner concerns or incidents documented as needed
  • follow-up items communicated to program leadership when necessary

Program Integrity Reminder

Faculty may personalize delivery style, examples, and facilitation approach. However, instructors must not replace required content, reduce competency coverage, or introduce role-confusing instruction that undermines peer support professionalism.
